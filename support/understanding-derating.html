Critical Design Principle

Why proper derating is essential for reliable power supply operation

One of the most common causes of premature power supply failure is neglecting to derate. A power supply's maximum output power is not always available under all operating conditions. Derating is the practice of operating a component at less than its maximum rated power to ensure reliability. MORNSUN datasheets provide clear derating curves to guide this process.

Understanding and properly applying these curves is not optional‚Äîit is essential for designing a reliable, long-lasting product. Pushing a power supply to its absolute limits without considering temperature and input voltage variations is a recipe for field failures.

When in doubt, our FAE team can help you interpret derating curves for your specific application. Proper derating today prevents costly field failures tomorrow.

1. What is Temperature Derating?

Understanding thermal limitations and power reduction requirements

As the ambient temperature around a power supply increases, its ability to dissipate its own internal heat decreases. To prevent overheating, the maximum load you can draw from the supply must be reduced. This is shown in the "Temperature Derating Curve."

How Temperature Affects Power Output

Typically, a module like the LM75-20B24 might offer 100% of its rated load up to 50¬∞C. Above that, the curve will show a linear decrease. For example, at 70¬∞C, you might only be able to draw 60% of the maximum load.

  • Full Power Range: Typically available up to 50¬∞C ambient temperature
  • Linear Derating: Progressive power reduction above threshold temperature
  • Critical Temperature: Maximum operating temperature with minimal load
  • Design Margin: Always design for your system's worst-case internal ambient temperature

Key Design Rule

Always design for your system's worst-case internal ambient temperature. The temperature inside your enclosure will typically be higher than room temperature due to heat generated by other components.

2. What is Input Voltage Derating?

Managing power limitations at low input voltages

Some power supplies, especially wide-input DC/DC converters, may need to be derated at the lower end of their input voltage range. This is because to deliver the same output power with a lower input voltage, the input current must increase (P = V √ó I). This higher current can cause additional stress and heating on the input-side components.

Input Voltage Derating Factors

  • Current Stress: Higher input current at low voltages increases component stress
  • Thermal Effects: Additional heating from increased current flow
  • Efficiency Impact: Lower efficiency at extreme input voltage ranges
  • Component Limits: Input side component ratings may limit maximum current

The "Input Voltage Derating Curve" will show if you need to reduce the maximum output power when operating near the minimum specified input voltage.

Wide-input range converters offer flexibility, but remember that maximum power may not be available across the entire input range. Check the derating curves for your specific operating conditions.

Practical Application Examples

Real-world scenarios and design calculations

Example 1: High-Temperature Industrial Application

Consider a 75W power module in an industrial control cabinet where the internal temperature can reach 65¬∞C:

  • Rated Power: 75W at 25¬∞C ambient
  • Operating Temperature: 65¬∞C inside enclosure
  • Derating Factor: 70% of rated power at 65¬∞C
  • Available Power: 75W √ó 0.70 = 52.5W maximum

Example 2: Automotive Application with Wide Input Range

An automotive DC/DC converter operating from 9V to 36V input, with 24V nominal:

  • Normal Operation: Full power available at 24V input
  • Cold Cranking: Input voltage drops to 9V during engine start
  • Derating Required: 80% of rated power at 9V input
  • Design Consideration: Size power supply for worst-case conditions

Design Best Practices

Guidelines for reliable power system design

Essential Design Rules

  • Conservative Derating: Apply 20-30% additional margin beyond datasheet requirements
  • Thermal Management: Design adequate cooling for your operating environment
  • Input Range Planning: Consider all possible input voltage variations
  • Environmental Testing: Test at maximum expected temperature and minimum input voltage
  • Documentation: Document your derating calculations for future reference

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ignoring Thermal Rise: Using room temperature instead of actual operating temperature
  • Optimistic Planning: Designing for typical rather than worst-case conditions
  • Single Point Analysis: Not considering combined effects of temperature and voltage
  • Inadequate Margins: Operating too close to maximum derated values
